\section{Downsampling}

We aim to perform the Fast Fourier Transorm (FFT) on our samples to detect what 
is the frequency component with the maximum power, i.e. the fundamental frequency.
But what resolution can we achieve? What is the range of frequencies we can measure?

When we fill the FFT buffer, we are capturing a specific ``window'' of time. 
If we collect $N$ samples, and take a new sample $f_s$ times per second, the total time it takes to fill that buffer is:
\begin{equation}
    T = \frac{N}{f_s}
    \label{equation:time_window}
\end{equation}
The FFT assumes that the chunk of data we captured is one repeating cycle.
Therefore, the absolute lowest frequency the FFT can see (other than 0~Hz DC) is a wave that completes exactly one full cycle inside our time window $T$.
Thus: 
\begin{equation}
    f_{\min} = \frac{1}{T}
    \label{equation:fmin}
\end{equation}

Substituting Equation~\ref{equation:time_window} into Equation~\ref{equation:fmin}, we get:
\begin{equation}
    f_{\min} = \frac{f_s}{N} = \Delta f
    \label{equation:resolution}
\end{equation}
\textit{Since every bin of the FFT is spaced between one another in multiples of $\Delta f$.} 
\paragraph{}

We derived the equation that tells us the resolution \textbf{and} the minimum frequency detectable, but what about the maximum?
The Nyquist theorem tells us that the maximum frequency we can detect through sampling is:
\begin{equation}
    f_s > 2 \cdot f_{\max}
    \label{equation:Nyquist}
\end{equation}
So with $N$ fixed, to get a finer resolution (smaller $\Delta f$), we want to \textbf{lower} $f_s$, 
but not too much; otherwise, we will not be able to detect some needed frequencies according to Equation~\ref{equation:Nyquist}.
\textit{(The role of $N$ will be discussed in Section~\ref{section:fft})}

\paragraph{}

Here is where \textbf{downsampling} comes into play: we can decide how many samples to keep to achieve 
a certain resolution (instead of changing the sample rate, which would be inconvenient).
But instead of discarding 9 samples out of 10, we accumulate those 10 values into a register and use that 
as our downsampled value.
\paragraph{}
This method also helps prevent spikes given by physical events (like picking a string)
by effectively computing an \textit{unscaled average}.
(Since we are looking for the biggest value in the FFT, we do not need to scale it back down).

\paragraph{}
Moreover, it helps with another problem, \textbf{aliasing}: the Nyquist theorem (Equation~\ref{equation:Nyquist}) also 
tells us that if the guitar produces some high frequency noises (which it does), the low $f_s$ will produce some low-frequency
noise into the processed data. If you just dropped 9 out of 10 samples, all the frequencies multiple of $f_s$ would alias
to 0 Hz (DC), giving power to the incorrect bin.
A visual explanation can be found at dsprelated.com\cite{dsprelated}.
\paragraph{}
The accumulation helps because it acts as a \textbf{low pass filter}:\\
In signal processing, summing $N$ subsequent samples is the same as \textbf{convolving} your
audio with a ``rectangular window''\cite{dsp}, which in the frequency domain has a frequency response
given by the $sinc$ function:
\begin{equation}
    H(f)=\frac{\sin(\pi f M T_s)}{\sin(\pi f T_s)}
    \label{equation:FR}
\end{equation}  

\textit{Where $M$ is the downsample factor (10), and $T_s$ is the sample period.}

This solves completely the ``alias to 0'' problem, becuase the Equation~\ref{equation:FR} has zeroes at multiples of $f_s$.
Moreover, the low-frequencies pass through with maximum gain($M=10$), while the higher frequencies are attenuated.
For example, at our Nyquist limit:
\begin{equation}
    H(2400) = 6.39
\end{equation}
\textit{Which is $\approx63\%$ of maximum low-frequency amplitude.}

\section{The ping pong buffer}

To prevent data loss while the FFT component is elaborating the data,
we set up two buffers of 8192 entries using BRAM, and when one is full we feed it to the FFT 
and start pushing new samples into the other buffer.
\paragraph{}
Since the effective $f_s$ after downsampling is $4.8kHz$, then the time it takes to full one buffer is:
\begin{equation}
    T = \frac{8192}{f_s} \approx 1.7 s
\end{equation}

Most of the time taken by the whole pipeline is waiting on the data to be sampled.

\section{The FFT IP}\label{section:fft}

To perfrom the FFT we use the Xilinx component \texttt{Fast Fourier Transorm 9.1}. Reported here are 
the most important settings:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Size ($N$) at 8192 bins 
    \item Pipelined, streaming I/O
\end{itemize}
With these setting it uses 5185 LUTs, 34.5 BRAMs and 48 DSPs, and is able to do a full transformation in $247.15 \mu s$.
The output of the FFT are the frequencies real and complex components values spread across 8192 bins. 
$N$ was chosen to be 8192 because it is a good compromise between logic area and \textbf{high} frequency resolution.\\
The Pipelined version was chosen over the Radix burst version only because it is much easier to control. We still are collecting
the whole frame and then sending it to the FFT rather than sending a new sample whenever it comes, so switching to a Radix burst
version can be done with not much struggle.\\
Since we have area to spare we can't keep it that way. Quoting the most famous sentence in engineering: \textit{if it works, don't touch it!}

\section{Smart peak finder}

To find the fundamental frequency we want to find the most powerful component.
First problem is that the resulting 8192 bins, having the sample rate at 4800 hz, are:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Bins 0 to 4095 represent the true positive frequencies from 0 Hz to 2,400 Hz (Nyquist Limit).
    \item Bins 4096 to 8191 represent the negative frequencies wrapping backwards from 4,800 Hz down to 2,400 Hz.
\end{itemize}
\paragraph{}
Because the spectrum is mirrored, the peak energy is split equally in half.
If one plays a 440 Hz tone, the FFT produces a massive spike at Bin 751 (+440 Hz),
but it produces an identical massive spike at Bin 7441 (which is 4,800 Hz - 440 Hz = 4360 Hz)
That's why we only search for the bins up to 4096.

\paragraph{}

But here comes another problem: when playing the notes on a guitar, you don't only get the fundamental
frequency, you also get multiples of it, the \textbf{harmonics}, which sometimes can have
an higher power than the fundamental, breaking the calculation.
To get around this we implemented an algorithm called \textbf{Harmonic Product Spectrum (HPS)}:

\paragraph{}

We hold 3 BRAMs, that will contatin all the $real$ samples of $f_s$, $2f_s$, $3f_s$ respectively of sizes 4096, 2048, 1366.
When the data comes out of the FFT block, it puts all the bins in the first BRAM, one in two bins in the second and
one in three for the third BRAM.
As a result, the harmonics will line up at the same index, so we can multiply them together and finally

check for the \textbf{highest power}. A visual example of this can be seen in Figure~\ref{fig:hps}

\begin{figure}[h]
    \caption{Example of HPS result. \textit{Credits: sv.mazurka.org.uk}}
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.5\textwidth]{figures/hps.png}
    \label{fig:hps}
\end{figure}

There are different problems with this approach:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item The full power calculation of the value of the bin takes 64 bits, and multiplying three of them together
    would result in a 128 bits value, taking way too many DSP slices over the ZedBoard and risking timing violations.
    \item The operations of calcultating the power and the HPS cannot be done in the same clock cycle: multiplication takes a long time.
\end{enumerate}
For the \textbf{first} problem we first calculate the power with the standard formula, with the difference of avoiding the square root,
since we only care about the maximum, there's no need to square it back down:
\begin{equation}
    P = Re^2 + Im^2
\end{equation}
And from this 64 bits value we extract 16 bits, those between 48$^{th}$ and 32$^{nd}$, which are the
ones that carry the most useful information for us.
If the full power value is bigger than $2^{48}$ we set the 16 bits to 1s. If instead the full power 
value is smaller than $2^{32}$ then we set the 16-bit value to 1 to avoid breaking the HPS multiplication.
\paragraph{}
For the \textbf{second} problem we set up a ``conveyor belt'': a 4 stage pipeline for the \textbf{write}:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Split real part and imaginary part.
    \item Square each.
    \item Add them together.
    \item Extract the 16 bit value.
\end{enumerate}
and a 3-stage pipeline for the \textbf{HPS}:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Multply value in BRAM1 with value in BRAM2.
    \item Multply last result with value in BRAM3.
    \item Compare and save the peak.
\end{enumerate}
